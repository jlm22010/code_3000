## Personal Ethics Statement

My approach to ethics in computing is shaped by a core belief: software is speech, and the choices engineers make about openness, transparency, and control are inherently moral ones.

Chapter 5 of *Ethical Machines* reinforced something I already held; opacity in systems, whether in AI models or proprietary black boxes, isn't a neutral technical decision. They are choices made, which concentrate power and erodes accountability. Engineers who build systems without considering who controls them, who can audit them, and who bears the cost when they fail are making an ethical choice by omission.

I try to follow my belief concretely. I default to GPL v3 for my projects not as a formality (no one follows or abides by the license anyway, one of my projects (cortex-vm)[https://github.com/jonahmer22/cortex-vm] has 3k clones but no activity other than me so those surely have to be someone training an AI or some form of data collection on my code which, as far as I am concerned, violates the license) but as a statement; software I write should remain free for the people who use it. I prefer writing core logic myself not just out of craft preference, but because depending on opaque dependencies shifts responsibility without eliminating it.

The hardest part of engineering ethics isn't identifying obvious harms; it's resisting the normalization of small compromises: closed formats, silent data collection, interfaces designed to obscure rather than inform. The ethical obligation of a computer scientist is to ask who a system serves; when the answer isn't the user that should prompt reflection.
